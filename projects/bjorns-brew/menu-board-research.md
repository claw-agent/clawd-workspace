## 1. Psychology of Menu Design

### 1.1 The Golden Triangle

When customers view a menu, their eyes follow a predictable pattern known as the **"Golden Triangle"**:

1. **Center** â€“ Eyes move here first
2. **Top Right** â€“ Second area of focus
3. **Top Left** â€“ Third area of focus

**Source:** Aaron Allen & Associates, "The Psychology of Restaurant Menu Design"

**Actionable Recommendation:** Place your highest-margin, signature drinks in these three zones. For a coffee shop menu board, consider placing specialty espresso drinks or seasonal offerings in the center, with premium coffee selections at top right.

### 1.2 Eye Movement Patterns by Menu Configuration

According to menu engineer Gregg Rapp:

| Menu Type | Customer Behavior | Engineering Impact |
|-----------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **One-panel** | Faster decisions, lower ordering volume | Lower profitability per customer |
| **Two-panel** | Optimal balance; feels like full experience | Best for menu engineering |
| **Three-panel** | Valid for many items, harder to read | Moderate engineering control |
| **Many-panel** | Overwhelming; customers default to familiar items | Least control over decisions |

**Source:** Menu Cover Depot, "Menu Engineering: How to Raise Restaurant Profits 15% or More"

**Actionable Recommendation:** For a coffee shop menu board, aim for a **two-panel visual structure** even on a single boardâ€”divide into clear left/right or top/bottom sections.

### 1.3 The Burden of Choice (Decision Fatigue)

When confronted with too many options, customers:
- Have difficulty deciding
- Are more likely to be unsatisfied with their choice
- Default to the most common/familiar item (which is often the least profitable)

**Optimal number of items per section: 5-7 items**

**Source:** Lightspeed HQ, "Menu Engineering: How to Make a Profitable Restaurant Menu"

**Actionable Recommendation:** Limit each menu category (espresso drinks, drip coffee, specialty drinks, food) to **7 items maximum**. Consider rotating seasonal items rather than adding to the permanent menu.

### 1.4 The Power of Positioning

- **Top 2 items** in each section are ordered most frequently
- **Last item** in each section gets significant attention
- **Middle items** (positions 3-5 in a 6-item list) receive the least attention

**Source:** Menu Cover Depot, Gregg Rapp interview

**Actionable Recommendation:** 
- Position **highest-margin drinks first** in each category
- Place your **third-most-profitable item last**
- Use middle positions for "plow-horses" (popular but lower-margin items)

## 2. Typography and Readability

### 2.1 Core Typography Principles for Menu Boards

**Font Selection Guidelines:**

| Element | Recommendation | Rationale |
|---------|---------------|-----------|
| **Headlines/Categories** | Sans-serif, bold weight | Maximum legibility at distance |
| **Item Names** | Sans-serif or readable serif | Clear distinction from descriptions |
| **Descriptions** | Sans-serif, regular weight | Readability; avoid script fonts |
| **Prices** | Same font as descriptions | Reduces price emphasis |

**Recommended Sans-Serif Families:**
- Helvetica / Helvetica Neue
- Gotham
- Proxima Nova
- Futura
- Avenir

### 2.2 Viewing Distance Calculations

**Rule of Thumb:** For every 10 feet of viewing distance, increase font size by 1 inch for primary text.

| Viewing Distance | Minimum Font Height |
|-----------------|---------------------|
| 10 feet | 1 inch |
| 20 feet | 2 inches |
| 30 feet | 3 inches |

**Contrast Requirements:**
- Minimum contrast ratio of **4.5:1** for standard text
- **7:1** for optimal readability
- Dark text on light background typically provides better readability than reverse

### 2.3 Hierarchy and Spacing

**Visual Hierarchy (in order of prominence):**
1. Category headers (largest, boldest)
2. Item names
3. Prices
4. Descriptions (if space allows)

**Spacing Guidelines:**
- **Line spacing:** 120-150% of font size
- **Category separation:** 2x the spacing between items
- **Padding around highlighted items:** Equal on all sides

**Source:** Typography best practices compiled from design standards

**Actionable Recommendation:** For a typical coffee shop counter with 10-15 foot viewing distance:
- Category headers: 2-3 inches
- Item names: 1-1.5 inches
- Prices: 0.75-1 inch

## 4. Pricing Presentation Strategies

### 4.1 The #1 Mistake: Price Columns

**Never list prices in a column on the right side of the menu.**

This causes customers to:
- Focus on price instead of offerings
- Scan for the cheapest option
- Choose based on cost rather than desire

**Source:** Menu Cover Depot; Aaron Allen & Associates

### 4.2 Optimal Price Presentation

**Recommended Techniques:**

1. **Nested Pricing:** Place price 2 spaces after description, same font size and style
   - Example: "House Blend - Single origin Ethiopian, bright and fruity 4.50"

2. **Remove Dollar Signs:** Studies show removing "$" can increase spending up to 30%
   - Use: "4.50" not "$4.50"
   - Never write out "dollars"

3. **Strategic Rounding:**
   - **Charm pricing** (4.95, 4.99): Signals value/deals
   - **Round numbers** (5.00, 6.00): Signals premium quality
   - For specialty coffee shops, round numbers often work better

**Source:** Aaron Allen & Associates, "Psychology of Menu Design"

### 4.3 The Decoy Effect

Place a slightly more expensive item at the top of a category to make other items appear more reasonably priced.

**Example Structure:**
- Premium Single-Origin Pour-Over    6.50
- House Blend Drip Coffee            3.75
- Coffee of the Day                  3.25

The premium option makes the house blend seem like excellent value.

**Source:** Aaron Allen & Associates; behavioral economics research

### 4.4 Price Anchoring for Coffee Shops

Create clear price tiers that guide customers toward middle-priced (typically highest-margin) options:

| Tier | Example | Purpose |
|------|---------|---------|
| **Premium** | $6-7 | Anchors perception; attracts enthusiasts |
| **Standard** | $4-5 | Target tier; best margins |
| **Entry** | $2.50-3.50 | Accessibility; converts new customers |

## 5. Coffee Shop Menu Board Best Practices

### 5.1 Coffee-Specific Design Considerations

**Unique Challenges:**
- Counter ordering (customers need to decide quickly)
- Often viewed from a queue (varying distances)
- Morning rush = decision fatigue amplification
- Many similar-sounding drinks (latte, cappuccino, flat white)

**Solutions:**

1. **Clear Categorization:**
   - Espresso Drinks
   - Drip Coffee / Filter
   - Cold Drinks
   - Specialty / Seasonal
   - Food

2. **Size Pricing Clarity:**
   - Display all sizes with prices
   - Or indicate base size with "+$X for large"

3. **Modifiers Section:**
   - Alternative milks
   - Extra shots
   - Flavor additions

### 5.2 What Top Coffee Shops Do

Based on industry analysis:

| Practice | Adoption Rate | Impact |
|----------|--------------|--------|
| **Signature drink highlighting** | High | Creates differentiation; improves margins |
| **Limited menu** (under 25 items) | Growing | Faster decisions; better experience |
| **Seasonal rotation** | Standard | Drives repeat visits; generates buzz |
| **Visual hierarchy** | Essential | Reduces confusion; speeds ordering |
| **Story/origin information** | Premium shops | Justifies pricing; builds connection |

### 5.3 Digital vs. Static Menu Boards

**Digital Menu Board Benefits:**
- **28% higher ROI** on average vs. static boards
- Easy updates for seasonal items, out-of-stock items
- Dynamic pricing (e.g., happy hour)
- Visual appeal with motion/images

**When Digital Works Best:**
- High-traffic locations
- Frequently changing menus
- Multiple dayparts (breakfast, lunch, etc.)

**When Static Works Best:**
- Artisan/craft positioning
- Budget constraints
- Simple, unchanging menu
- Small spaces

**Source:** Aaron Allen & Associates (Burger King case study: 64% sales increase with digital boards)

**Actionable Recommendation:** If budget allows, consider a hybrid approachâ€”digital for specials/seasonal items, static for core menu items.

## 7. Visual Merchandising Techniques

### 7.1 Eye Magnets

Use graphic elements to draw attention to specific items:

- **Boxes/Frames:** Increase item sales up to **30%** when used sparingly
- **Icons:** â˜… for signatures, ðŸƒ for plant-based, â„ for cold drinks
- **Color blocks:** Highlight background for featured items
- **Photography:** Can increase sales 30% (but may cheapen premium perception)

**Critical Rule:** Use only **1-2 eye magnets per panel/section**. Overuse negates the effect.

**Source:** Gregg Rapp, Menu Engineers

### 7.2 Negative Space (White Space)

Items set apart in their own space receive significantly more attention than items crowded together.

**Application:**
- Surround signature drinks with extra padding
- Don't fill every inch of the menu board
- Allow eye rest between sections

### 7.3 Material and Presentation

The physical material communicates brand values:

| Material | Signal | Best For |
|----------|--------|----------|
| **Chalkboard** | Artisan, handcrafted, fresh | Local/independent shops |
| **Wood/natural materials** | Authentic, warm, sustainable | Nordic/craft positioning |
| **Matte black/white** | Modern, premium, clean | Contemporary specialty |
| **Backlit acrylic** | Professional, bright, clear | High-traffic, larger spaces |
| **Digital screens** | Dynamic, tech-forward | Fast-casual, chains |

**Actionable Recommendation:** For Bjorn's Brew's Nordic positioning, consider:
- Natural wood frame or backing
- Matte finishes (not glossy)
- Clean, minimalist layout
- Perhaps a hand-lettered accent for seasonal items

## 8. Descriptions and Copywriting

### 8.1 The Power of Descriptions

Properly written descriptions can increase sales of specific items by **27%** or more.

**Effective Description Elements:**
- **Sensory adjectives:** "velvety," "bold," "bright," "smooth"
- **Origin stories:** "Single-origin Ethiopian Yirgacheffe"
- **Process details:** "slow-roasted," "cold-brewed for 18 hours"
- **Emotional triggers:** "perfect for a cold morning"

### 8.2 Description Guidelines for Menu Boards

Given space constraints on menu boards:

| Item Type | Description Approach |
|-----------|---------------------|
| **Signature items** | 4-8 words; sensory + unique element |
| **Standard items** | 2-4 words; basic ingredients |
| **Specialty items** | Full origin/story (on separate card if needed) |

## 9. Testing and Iteration

### 9.1 The Importance of Testing

Menu engineering is not a one-time project. Leading restaurants:
- Review menu performance **twice per year minimum**
- A/B test positioning and descriptions
- Track item-level sales data
- Adjust based on changing costs and trends

### 9.2 Key Metrics to Track

| Metric | What It Tells You | Target |
|--------|-------------------|--------|
| **Sales per item** | Popularity | Identify stars and dogs |
| **Gross margin per item** | Profitability | Guide pricing decisions |
| **Category mix** | Balance | No category >40% of sales |
| **Average ticket** | Upselling success | Track post-menu changes |
| **Speed of service** | Menu complexity impact | Monitor during rush |

### 9.3 Staff Training

Your menu board is only as effective as your staff's ability to sell it.

**Training Requirements:**
- Every team member should taste signature items
- Staff should know top 3 items to recommend
- Train on upselling (sizes, add-ons, pairings)
- Weekly "menu moment" in team meetings

**Source:** Aaron Allen & Associates; Menu Cover Depot
